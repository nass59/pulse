That's the whole first chapter in one image. The reason you can't google "best way to split services" or "correct source of truth" isn't that architects are hiding the answer — it's that any problem generic enough to have a best practice has already been solved, wrapped in a library, and taken off your desk. What's left, by definition, is the hard parts: problems novel enough that nobody has seen your exact track conditions. The job isn't to find the right answer; it's to weigh trade-offs honestly and pick the **least-worst combination for your context** — then write down why.

The book grounds every chapter in a fictional consulting saga, the Sysops Squad. This track has something better than fiction: the system next door. Every chapter here gets tested against what Pulse actually built.

## One question, three answers

If best practices existed, the most fundamental question in an event-driven system — *where does the truth live?* — would have one answer, and Pulse asked it three times.

<SourceOfTruthSplit />

Look at what decided each column, because none of it is ideology — it's track conditions.

`identity` keeps **Postgres as the canonical store** because its data is full of promises that need a referee: a channel slug must be unique, a channel can have at most one live stream. Promises like that need something that can say *no, right now* — a unique index rejecting the second go-live in the same millisecond. An eventually-consistent log can't referee; it can only report. So the log gets demoted to a broadcast channel, fed through [the transactional outbox](/kafka/concepts/transactional-outbox) so state and announcement can never disagree.

`chat` flips it: **Kafka *is* the truth.** A chat message isn't a row that gets updated — it's a fact about the past. Alice said "gg" at 20:31:04, as [the gateway saw it](/kafka/concepts/server-authored-events), and nothing will ever make that less true. Facts want a log. Everything readable — the ring buffer that fills your screen mid-stream, the archives — is a projection rebuilt by replaying it.

`analytics` goes further and owns **no original state at all**. Its viewer counts live in state stores that are just materialized views over the other two services' topics. If a store dies, nothing is lost that a replay can't rebuild.

Three services, three sources of truth — deliberately, and recorded with reasoning at the very start of the project. That's not indecision. That's Monza and Monaco.

## Decisions with receipts

The chapter's most practical advice is also its cheapest: when you make an architectural decision, write down *why* — the context, the options you rejected, the trade-off you accepted — in a short **architecture decision record**. Not documentation for documentation's sake: the *what* of a decision is visible in the code forever, but the *why* evaporates in about six weeks.

Pulse has twenty-five of these records, and this very page exists because of one. Better: this pillar's own name is proof the habit pays. When this docs site was reorganized into pillars, the name *Distributed Systems* was considered for the Kafka pillar and rejected — **with the reasoning written down**. Months later, a shelf of architecture books needed a home, someone re-read that rejection, and the recorded *why* showed the name had been waiting for this pillar all along. A decision log doesn't just explain the past; it hands ammunition to the future.

<Callout variant="lesson" title="The why outlives the what">
  Six weeks after any decision, the code still shows *what* you did — three
  services, three truth models — but only a written record remembers that you
  weighed alternatives and what they would have cost. Without it, the next
  person (usually you) either re-litigates the decision or, worse, "fixes" it.
</Callout>

## Pulse's one fitness function — running at half value

The chapter's other tool is the **architecture fitness function**: an automated check that a structural quality you care about still holds — no cyclic dependencies, layers respected, contracts unbroken. Not a unit test (those check behaviour); a test *of the architecture itself*.

Pulse has exactly one thing that qualifies, and it's a good one. Every event schema is registered against the schema registry with its compatibility rule pinned to `BACKWARD` — so a schema change that would break existing consumers is *structurally rejected*, loudly, by machinery rather than by code review vigilance. That is a fitness function by the book's own definition: an automated check on an architectural characteristic, here the evolvability of contracts.

And here's the confession: **it only runs when a human remembers to run it.** The check lives in a script (`packages/schemas/scripts/publish.ts`), and there is no CI pipeline in this repo yet — nothing runs it on every change. The book is blunt about exactly this: a fitness function delivers its full value only when it runs *continually*, because the whole point is catching the erosion you weren't looking for.

<Callout variant="footgun" title="A manual fitness function is a smoke alarm you have to remember to sniff">
  The gate works — a breaking schema gets rejected. But it guards the moment
  someone runs the script, not the moment someone makes the mistake. The gap
  between those two moments is where a broken contract slips in and waits.
  Half-built on purpose is fine; half-built and *documented as finished* is
  how systems rot — the glossary here claimed "CI" until this chapter forced
  the correction.
</Callout>

## Operational vs analytical — the trailer

The chapter plants one more distinction it will grow later: **operational data** (the rows a business inserts, updates, and deletes to function — OLTP) versus **analytical data** (the read-optimized views the business learns from — OLAP). Pulse doesn't need to imagine that split; it's physically two services. `identity`'s Postgres is the operational end — every follow and go-live is a transaction. `analytics` is the analytical end — windowed aggregates nobody ever updates, only recomputes. And the bridge between the two worlds is the log itself.

The book digs into this properly in its data chapters; this section fills in when the reading gets there.

## The road not taken

The honest way to respect a trade-off is to price the option you rejected. So: what would *one* truth model everywhere cost Pulse?

Force `chat`'s answer onto `identity` — event-source everything, Kafka as the only truth — and every promise turns into a race. "Is this slug taken?" becomes a read against a log that might not have caught up with the write that took it. The one-live-stream rule loses its referee: two go-lives a millisecond apart both look fine to a consumer that hasn't seen either yet. You'd end up rebuilding synchronous coordination *on top of* the log — badly.

Force `identity`'s answer onto `chat` — Postgres canonical, log as broadcast — and every chat message pays a database round-trip before anyone sees it, on a service built to hold thousands of live sockets per node. The write path that made go-live safe becomes the bottleneck that makes chat slow, guarding invariants chat doesn't have. A message needs no referee; it's already true.

Each transplant fails for the same reason the original choices work: the constraints — the track — are different. The differences between the three columns aren't inconsistency. They're the design.

## What would change our mind

- **A CI pipeline lands in the repo.** The schema gate gets wired into it that week — it's a one-script job waiting for a place to run — and Pulse's fitness function starts earning its other half.
- **A second fitness function suggests itself.** The moment services share internal libraries, a cyclic-dependency check (the chapter's own example) becomes worth automating. Today, three services that only meet through Kafka topics can't have that disease.
- **The book reaches its analytical-data chapters.** The trailer section above gets replaced by the real thing, tested against how `analytics` actually windows, stores, and serves.
