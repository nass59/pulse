Spin up a Kafka cluster and *someone* has to keep the books: which brokers are alive right now, what topics exist, and who's the leader for each partition. Picture a football squad — somebody has to hold the roster and the starting eleven, or nobody knows who's on the pitch. Kafka calls that node the **controller**.

<ControllerRole />

## For years, the clerk lived off the team

Here's the strange part: for most of Kafka's life, the controller's books *didn't live on the cluster*. Kafka hired an outside agency — a whole separate **ZooKeeper** ensemble — just to hold the metadata. That's two distributed systems to deploy, secure, upgrade and reason about, when you only ever wanted one.

**KRaft** (Kafka + Raft) fires the clerk. One broker is elected controller and keeps the roll itself, in an internal log replicated across the voters by the **Raft** consensus algorithm. Pulse runs KRaft from day one; ZooKeeper never enters the picture.

<KraftVsZookeeper />

In `infra/docker-compose.yaml` the single broker is told to play both parts — the team *and* its scorekeeper:

```yaml
KAFKA_PROCESS_ROLES: broker,controller
KAFKA_CONTROLLER_QUORUM_VOTERS: 1@kafka:9094
```

## Watch one broker elect itself

The single most useful thing to feel locally: the broker is **not instantly ready**. It boots, reads its metadata log, runs an election, and only *then* can serve. With one voter that election is over in a blink — but it still happens. Run the sequence:

## Why one node is still a "quorum"

Raft elects a leader by **majority vote** — picture a crew voting someone out in Among Us, or bandmates naming a frontman: you need more than half to agree before it sticks. `KAFKA_CONTROLLER_QUORUM_VOTERS: 1@kafka:9094` declares a quorum of exactly one voter, so the majority is one and the node wins instantly, unopposed. No contention, no split-brain to resolve.

The *same* mechanism, with 3 or 5 voters, runs a genuine election and replicates the metadata log across them — which is how a real cluster survives losing a controller. The quorum size is the only dial that changes:

<Callout variant="lesson" title="Single-node is a real quorum, just a trivial one">
  Pulse runs one voter because this is a local learning cluster, not a
  production deployment. The election you watch above is genuine; the quorum is
  simply small enough to be uncontested. Scaling the voter list is the *only*
  change needed to make it a fault-tolerant multi-node Raft group — no
  architectural shift, the same code path.
</Callout>

## The boot delay is why the healthcheck waits

Those few seconds between "container started" and "controller elected" are exactly why Kafka's healthcheck carries `start_period: 20s`. Without it, the probe would mark the container `unhealthy` during a perfectly normal boot. That mechanism is its own concept — see [Healthchecks](/concepts/healthchecks).
