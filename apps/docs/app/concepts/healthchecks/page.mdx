<Hook>
  a service that's "started" isn't always "ready." Tell the next service to wait
  for the wrong one and your stack races itself on boot.
</Hook>

Think of opening a restaurant. The lights are on and the door's unlocked (*the process is running*) — but the ovens are still cold and nobody's prepped. You don't want diners walking in yet. A Docker **healthcheck** is the chef sticking their head out to say "actually ready now."

It's a command the container runs on a schedule to answer one question: *can this service actually do its job right now?* Not "is the process up" — Docker already knows that — but "can it serve." All four pieces of Pulse's infrastructure declare one:

| Service | Probe | What it proves |
|---|---|---|
| kafka | `kafka-topics.sh --list` | the broker answers metadata requests |
| postgres | `pg_isready -U postgres -d pulse` | the database accepts connections |
| redis | `redis-cli ping` | the server responds to commands |
| apicurio | `curl /health/ready` | the registry is ready to serve schemas |

The distinction matters because a process can be *up* long before it is *useful*. Postgres has to finish recovery; Kafka has to elect a controller. A healthcheck is the difference between "started" and "ready," and downstream services that declare `depends_on: { condition: service_healthy }` wait for *ready*, not merely *started*.

## The `start_period` gotcha

Here is the subtlety that bites everyone once. A healthcheck has an `interval`, a `timeout`, and a `retries` count — fail `retries` times in a row and the container is marked `unhealthy`. But a slow-booting service will *legitimately* fail its first few probes while it is still coming up. Without protection, a normal boot looks like a failure.

`start_period` is that protection. It defines a grace window after start during which **failing probes do not count toward `retries`**. The probe still runs; a failure just doesn't accrue. Once the service passes once, or the window expires, normal counting begins.

<HealthcheckTimeline />

```yaml
healthcheck:
  test: ["CMD", "/opt/kafka/bin/kafka-topics.sh", "--bootstrap-server", "localhost:9092", "--list"]
  interval: 10s
  timeout: 5s
  retries: 5
  start_period: 20s
```

Kafka's window is `20s` because single-node KRaft needs a handful of seconds to elect itself controller before `--list` can succeed. You can watch exactly that sequence — the failing probes inside the window, then the flip to green — on the **[KRaft mode](/concepts/kraft-mode)** page.

<Callout variant="note">
  `start_period` was added to Compose healthchecks precisely for services with a
  meaningful warm-up. If you find yourself bumping `retries` to paper over boot
  time, you almost certainly want `start_period` instead — it keeps the
  steady-state failure threshold honest.
</Callout>
